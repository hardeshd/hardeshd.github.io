Dr Hardesh Dhillon is an Interventional Gastroenterologist currently completing a third advanced endoscopy fellowship year at Singapore General Hospital, focusing on EUS and ERCP at one of Asia's leading quaternary centres.

His training spans Australia and Japan — including a JSGE-scholarship-funded fellowship at Kyoto Prefectural University of Medicine and advanced endoscopy roles at Northern Health, Barwon Health, and Monash Health — building competency across general gastroenterology and all major therapeutic luminal and hepatobiliary procedures.

For Patients

Patient Information

The following information is intended to help you understand the procedures performed by Dr Dhillon. Please discuss any questions or concerns with your doctor before your procedure.

Gastroscopy & Colonoscopy▾

Upper & lower GI endoscopy

What is it?

Gastroscopy uses a thin flexible camera to examine the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum. Colonoscopy examines the large bowel. Both are performed under sedation and are safe, well-tolerated procedures.

Common Indications

  • Heartburn, reflux or indigestion
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Abdominal pain or bloating
  • Unexplained iron deficiency or anaemia
  • Rectal bleeding or change in bowel habits
  • Bowel cancer screening and surveillance
  • Polyp detection and removal

Risks

  • Bleeding (rare, usually minor)
  • Perforation of the bowel wall (very rare, <1 in 1000)
  • Reaction to sedation medications
  • Aspiration (inhaling stomach contents)
  • Missed lesions in a small proportion of cases
  • Temporary bloating or discomfort after the procedure
Endoscopic Mucosal Resection (EMR)▾

Lift and snare technique

What is it?

EMR is a minimally invasive technique to remove larger polyps or early cancers from the lining of the digestive tract without surgery. Fluid is injected beneath the lesion to lift it, then a snare is used to remove it.

Common Indications

  • Large bowel polyps not removable by standard techniques
  • Early colorectal cancer
  • Barrett's oesophagus with dysplasia
  • Early gastric lesions
  • Flat or sessile polyps

Risks

  • Bleeding — immediate or delayed (5–10%)
  • Perforation (<1%)
  • Incomplete resection requiring further treatment
  • Recurrence at the resection site
  • Post-polypectomy syndrome (fever, pain without perforation)
Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD)▾

En bloc knife dissection

What is it?

ESD is an advanced technique that allows removal of large or complex lesions in one piece (en bloc) using specialised electrosurgical knives. It offers lower recurrence rates than EMR for large lesions.

Common Indications

  • Early gastric cancer
  • Early colorectal cancer
  • Large or fibrotic polyps not suitable for EMR
  • Early oesophageal lesions
  • Lesions requiring en bloc resection for accurate pathology

Risks

  • Bleeding (~5%)
  • Perforation (~5%), usually managed endoscopically
  • Stricture formation (particularly oesophageal ESD)
  • Longer procedure time and anaesthetic exposure
  • Requires hospitalisation for observation
Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS)▾

Ultrasound from inside the gut

What is it?

EUS combines endoscopy with high-frequency ultrasound to produce detailed images of the digestive tract and nearby organs including the pancreas, bile ducts, and lymph nodes. It can also perform guided biopsies (FNA/FNB).

Common Indications

  • Pancreatic cysts and masses
  • Bile duct stones
  • Submucosal tumours
  • Lymph node assessment
  • Staging of gastrointestinal cancers
  • Evaluation of unexplained abdominal pain

Risks

  • Bleeding (rare)
  • Perforation (very rare)
  • Pancreatitis if FNA of pancreatic lesion (~1–2%)
  • Infection (rare, prophylactic antibiotics given for cyst FNA)
  • Sedation-related risks
ERCP▾

Bile duct access & treatment

What is it?

Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) combines endoscopy and X-ray to diagnose and treat conditions of the bile ducts and pancreatic duct, including stone removal and stent placement, without open surgery.

Common Indications

  • Bile duct stones causing jaundice or pancreatitis
  • Biliary obstruction or stricture
  • Unexplained jaundice
  • Bile duct stent placement
  • Post-surgical bile duct complications
  • Chronic pancreatitis with duct dilation

Risks

  • Post-ERCP pancreatitis (3–5%, most cases mild)
  • Bleeding after sphincterotomy (~1–2%)
  • Perforation (<1%)
  • Cholangitis or infection (1–3%)
  • Contrast dye reaction (rare)
  • Stent migration or blockage (if stent placed)
Full Thickness Resection▾

Full wall clip & resection

What is it?

Full Thickness Resection (FTR) removes all layers of the bowel wall to treat lesions that cannot be safely removed by conventional methods. A specialised over-the-scope device (FTRD) clips and resects the lesion in one step.

Common Indications

  • Difficult polyps at previous resection sites with scarring
  • Submucosal tumours
  • Appendiceal orifice lesions
  • Non-lifting polyps after prior EMR
  • T1 colorectal cancers in select cases

Risks

  • Bleeding (uncommon)
  • Perforation (contained by the clip in most cases)
  • Incomplete resection
  • Post-procedural pain or fever
  • Clip-related complications (rare)
Double Balloon Enteroscopy▾

Deep small bowel access

What is it?

Double Balloon Enteroscopy (DBE) is a specialised technique to examine and treat the small bowel, which is not accessible by standard endoscopy. Two balloons accordion the small intestine over the scope, allowing deep insertion and therapy.

Common Indications

  • Unexplained gastrointestinal bleeding
  • Small bowel tumours or polyps
  • Crohn's disease assessment and treatment
  • Abnormal capsule endoscopy findings
  • Small bowel stricture dilation

Risks

  • Perforation (higher risk than standard endoscopy, ~0.3–0.8%)
  • Bleeding
  • Pancreatitis (retrograde/oral approach, ~0.3%)
  • Prolonged procedure requiring deeper sedation or general anaesthesia
  • Abdominal discomfort or bloating after the procedure
